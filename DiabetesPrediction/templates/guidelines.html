Why Did We Choose These Features to Predict Diabetes?

Diabetes risk is influenced by various physiological and genetic factors. Each feature below plays a crucial role in assessing the likelihood of diabetes.

(If unsure about any value, enter 0.)

Input Parameters & Their Importance

1. Pregnancies

The number of times a woman has been pregnant affects hormonal balance, which can impact insulin resistance. Women with more pregnancies may have a higher risk of developing diabetes.

How to find it? Count the number of times you have been pregnant.

Values: Whole number (e.g., 0, 1, 2, etc.).

2. Glucose

Measures blood sugar levels after consuming glucose. Higher values indicate poor glucose metabolism, a major risk factor for diabetes.

How to find it? Get a fasting blood glucose test or an oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) at a lab.

Values: Number (e.g., 80, 120, 150).

3. Blood Pressure

High blood pressure is linked to metabolic disorders, including diabetes. It can also indicate poor circulation and heart health.

How to find it? Use a blood pressure monitor or visit a healthcare provider.

Values: Number (e.g., 70, 80, 90).

4. Skin Thickness

The thickness of the fold of skin and underlying fat on the back of the upper arm

How to measure?

Using skinfold calipers,pinch a fold of skin and read the thickness in millimeters

Values: Measured in Millimeters, Skin Thickness can typically range from 0 to 99mm

5. Insulin

Reflects how much insulin is in your blood after consuming glucose. Abnormal levels suggest insulin resistance.

How to find it? Get a blood insulin test at a lab.

Values: Number (e.g., 50, 100, 200).

6. Diabetes Pedigree Function

Estimates genetic risk based on family history of diabetes.

Example Calculation:

Your father has diabetes (diagnosed before age 45) → 0.5 × 1.2 = 0.6

Your father has diabetes (diagnosed after age 45) → 0.5 × 1.0 = 0.6

Your mother has diabetes (diagnosed before 55) → 0.5 × 1.0 = 0.5

Your mother has diabetes (diagnosed after 55) → 0.5 × 0.8 = 0.4

One grandparent (diagnosed at 60) → 0.25 × 1.0 = 0.25

One sibling (diagnosed at 40) → 0.5 × 1.2 = 0.6

Total DPF(based on the above given users can select and add ) = 0.6 + 0.5 + 0.25 + 0.6 = 1.95

Values: Decimal (e.g., 0.3, 0.5, 1.2).

7. Age

Older individuals are at a greater risk of diabetes due to metabolic changes and reduced insulin sensitivity over time.

Values: Whole number (e.g., 25, 40, 60).

SHAP Feature Contribution Explanation

After prediction, you will see factors influencing your result:

Positive values → Increase your diabetes risk.

Negative values → Reduce your diabetes risk.
